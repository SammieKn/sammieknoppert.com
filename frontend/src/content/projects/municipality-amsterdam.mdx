## The Challenge

Amsterdam has thousands of retaining walls lining its canals. Every few years,
divers go down to inspect them and come back up with thick PDF reports: tables of
measurements, defect codes, notes scratched together underwater. Someone has to
turn all of that into a structural risk assessment, named the ARK.

The brief from the Municipality of Amsterdam was half an a4, but boiled down to:
*"Use an LLM to read the tables and recognise the cracks."* Together with colleagues
from Arcadis and engineering consultancy Witteveen+Bos, we could have built exactly that in a
few weeks. Instead, we spent the first weeks figuring out what was actually broken.

I made a point of being on-site one day a week, not in meetings, no other work.
The engineers would open a PDF, manually copy measurements into a
spreadsheet, cross-reference defects, and make a
severity call. Twenty-four hours per report on average, not because they were
slow, but because the data was a mess and no one had ever defined what the
structure should be.

Crucially: **these engineers had no interest in AI making decisions for them.**
They wanted clean, structured data so they could make better decisions faster.

## The Solution

### The Data Model

Before writing a single prompt or line of code, we needed to define what we were
extracting. This is where having a** civil engineering background** became genuinely
useful. I could sit in a room with the municipality's structural experts and
actually follow the conversation: what a *rak* is, why a *GrondVoerendGat* carries
different risk than *LokaalVerdwenenMetselwerk*, why the connection between a pile
and its cap matters more than its diameter alone.

We designed a schema that mapped the physical reality of a retaining wall to
strict, validated fields. Not a loose JSON blob. A contract, enforced by Pydantic.

### Three Sprints

  src="/images/projects/municipality-amsterdam-sprints.webp"

We built in three sprints with one rule: don't increase complexity until the
previous step works on real data.
- Sprint 1 was the data model and shared vocabulary.
- Sprint 2 tested the riskiest hypothesis: can we actually extract clean structured data from these PDFs?
- Sprint 3 was the full pipeline with logging and tests, deployed on Viktor.

## Results

Analysis time dropped from 24 hours to 16 per report. Eight hours back, at €150/hr
for a senior structural engineer.

## What Came After

Honestly, this is one of the clients I enjoy working for most. The Municipality of
Amsterdam is doing genuinely innovative work in a sector that moves slowly by
design. They don't rush new technology. They roll things out
carefully, test thoroughly, and are almost unreasonably rigorous about checking.

With this project, we gave their stakeholders proof on the use of AI in their work,
now it is up to them to promote and implement it across the organisation. But I hope
to be involved in that process as well.

After this succesfull collaboration, they have asked me to make a new application,
the soft-shell calculator
based on TU Delft methodologies ([GitHub](https://github.com/SammieKn/soft-shell-calculator-ark)), and eventually to a bigger shift:
rather than coming in as a
vendor, the municipality brought me on as **Product Owner** for their Bridges and
Retaining Walls development toolings.

And I'll be honest: I live in Amsterdam. I cycle past these canals every week. The
retaining walls we worked on are ones I can point to from my bike, and I hope
there's a lot more of that to come.
